01The one weird rule

Every tokenizer refuses to
merge across a space

BPE learns merges greedily — t+h → th, th+e → the — but a hard pretokenization step splits text on whitespace first, so a merge can never cross a space.

Tokens are always subwords. "New York", "by the way", "of course" — each is forced into multiple tokens, forever.

Reasonable in 2016. But is whitespace really the boundary of meaning?

02The idea

Let tokens be
superwords

SuperBPE keeps BPE almost entirely intact. It just lifts the whitespace rule partway through training, so frequent multi-word phrases can become single tokens.

Why it should help

Phrases like by accident, of course, in the long run are fixed expressions. One token each = fewer tokens per document = more text per context window and cheaper inference.

a local change to tokenization, nothing else
03Mechanism

A two-phase curriculum

  • 1Phase 1 — subwords.Ordinary BPE with whitespace pretokenization ON. Learns base units up to a transition point t.
  • 2Phase 2 — superwords.Turn pretokenization OFF. Keep merging — now pairs can bridge whitespace. Continue to the final vocab T.
Learn the words first. Then learn the phrases.
one knob: where to flip the switch
04The knob: t

Two old algorithms are just the extremes

Transition pointWhat you getResult
t = Tflip switch never → plain BPEbaseline
t = 0no pretokenization ever → naive BPEworse — greedy makes bad early merges
0 < t < Tsubwords then superwords → SuperBPEbest — interior optimum

The paper finds the sweet spot is an interior value (their best is around t ≈ 80k of a 200k vocab). Learn the alphabet of words before gluing them together.

My sweep confirms it — vocab 16k

naive (t=0): 5.59  ·  t=0.6T: 5.80 (best)  ·  pure BPE (t=T): 4.91 bytes/token. Smooth curve, interior optimum — both extremes worse.

05Reproducing it

The phases fall out of one choice

I didn't fight a library. The whole two-phase trick reduces to what you train on: phase 1 trains on word units (merges can't escape a word); phase 2 trains on whole-document units (boundaries gone).

~250 lines, pure Python

Byte-level BPE + a transition point. Same merge engine for both phases — only the unit changes.

# phase 1: train on WORD units units = words # ["▁the","▁cat",…] train_bpe(units, upto=t) # merges stay in-word # transition: dissolve boundaries units = [concat(doc)] # whole doc, ▁ kept # phase 2: same engine, now # merges span spaces → superwords train_bpe(units, upto=T)

08Why it works

It eats the phrases BPE can't

At 32k vocab my SuperBPE learned 10,918 superwords. What are they? Fixed multi-word expressions:

▁of▁course▁by▁the▁way▁in▁the▁long▁run▁as▁well▁as▁for▁example
  • ▶BPE plateaus because it exhausts the useful whole-words and starts adding rare junk subwords.Bounded above by the average word length of the text.
  • ▶SuperBPE keeps winning by treating common word sequences as single units — there are far more of those to find.
09Honest limits

What I did not reproduce

✓ Reproduced

The encoding-efficiency mechanism: SuperBPE beats BPE, BPE plateaus, the gap widens with vocab. On a laptop, $0.

✗ Not attempted

The headline +8.2% MMLU at 8B params. At ~1M params downstream accuracy is pure noise — the paper says so too. Claiming it here would be dishonest.

Different corpus & vocab

My absolute bytes/token differ from the paper's (10 GB OLMo2 mix, 200k vocab). I reproduce the shape and direction, not the exact numbers — that's what's reproducible at this scale.
